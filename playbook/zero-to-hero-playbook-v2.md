**HOW TO USE THIS PLAYBOOK**

*Zero to Hero: Idea to App  |  Read this page first.*

**What this is:**

A complete production playbook for a first-time distributed documentary team. Every document you need — from the first pitch conversation to the final Netflix delivery — is in here, in the order you’ll use it. Nothing is missing. Nothing needs to be invented from scratch.

**What this isn’t:**

A rigid rulebook. This is a living document. When something doesn’t fit your situation, adapt it. The structure is here to serve the story, not the other way around.

**What’s new in v2.0:**

Thirteen new chapters added. Yash Javeri recast as Marketing & Content Strategist. A two-stage protagonist selection system replaces the single questionnaire. New chapters cover the async showcase protocol, narrator script, music brief, cut review process, release strategy, and team communication rhythm.

## **THE MOST IMPORTANT THINGS TO KNOW BEFORE YOU START**

* The Before Interview (Chapter 13\) must happen in M2 Week 1 before any transformation has occurred. It cannot be recreated. It is the film’s most powerful structural device.

* Eddie starts reviewing footage in M3 — not M5. The editor who waits for production wrap is always the editor who panics in post.

* Hansh sets up the folder structure (Chapter 11\) before a single clip is filmed. One file in the wrong folder is a file that takes an hour to find in M5.

* The Participant Consent Form (Chapter 8\) must be signed before anyone is filmed. No exceptions.

* Shefali sends a filming prompt every single Monday without fail. Every. Single. Week.

* Yash publishes content every week from M3. The documentary’s audience is built during production, not after.

* The Stage 1 questionnaire opt-in lines are not optional. No signed opt-in, no protagonist consideration. No exceptions.

## **A NOTE ON THIS PLAYBOOK ITSELF**

This playbook was built for a team of five people — most of them doing this for the first time — attempting something genuinely extraordinary: a distributed, self-shot, globally-cast documentary about transformation, made by people who are themselves in the middle of a transformation.

It is thorough because thoroughness is how a small team with no traditional crew infrastructure makes something that can stand next to professionally produced work. Every form, every log, every naming convention exists because someone, somewhere, on a production just like this, once had to find out the hard way why it mattered.

Use what you need. Adapt what doesn’t fit. And remember: the document is not the film. The film is what happens when you put down the playbook and press record.

# **Chapter 01 — THE PITCH**

## **FROM ZERO TO APP**

*How 30 Strangers From Six Countries Learned to Build with AI — and Why It Changed Everything*

A Feature Documentary  |  1 x 60 Minutes  |  100X Engineers Program  |  Directed by Munish Bhardwaj

**LOGLINE:**

200 adults with no technical background. One brutal online selection process. 30 survivors scattered across six countries, six time zones, and wildly different lives — all attempting the same impossible thing: build a working app using Generative AI in 12 weeks. One of the 30 is also making this film. Another is directing it.

## **THE META-STORY**

Most documentaries observe transformation from the outside. This one is different: the filmmaker is inside the story she is making. Shefali Mody — leadership coach, solopreneur, narrator — is one of the 30 cohort members. She is not an embedded observer. She is a participant, with her own fear, her own deadline, her own app to build.

Munish Bhardwaj, her co-creator and Director, is also from within this world — making his first documentary alongside a cohort making their first apps. The film they are making IS the transformation they are documenting.

## **THE STORY**

The 100X Engineers program accepts 200 applicants from around the world into a GenAI course promising to take complete beginners to a working, deployed app in 12 weeks. Most have never written a line of code. From those 200, a cohort of 30 find each other and become, unexpectedly and improbably, a family.

We follow five of them — five radically different humans from different countries, different decades, different starting points. And woven through all of it: one narrator who is not standing outside this story. She is living it.

## **WHY NOW**

AI is the most consequential democratisation of creative and economic power in a generation. This film shows what it looks like from the inside — from the terrified, hopeful, hilarious, human inside — and asks: what does it actually take to cross the digital divide when the bridge is being built in real time?

## **THE FIVE PROTAGONISTS**

| ROLE | WHO THEY ARE | CORE FEAR | TRANSFORMATION | NARRATIVE FUNCTION |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| The Director | Munish — making his first documentary alongside the cohort | What if I can’t capture something this important? | Becomes a filmmaker. | The meta-layer. |
| The Narrator-Filmmaker | Shefali — coach, solopreneur, IN the program AND making the film | Can I build anything real? | Ships an actual product. Changes how she sees herself. | Audience anchor. Her doubt is our doubt. |
|  |  |  |  |  |

| The Wildcard (TBD) | Defies a core assumption — from questionnaire. | TBD — selected because their story subverts something. | TBD. The surprise. | Every great documentary has one. |

## **THE FORMAT**

Primary footage: self-shot iPhone footage by participants using a weekly guided protocol. Supplemented by Zoom interviews recorded via double-ender method. No traditional film crew. The raw, unpolished quality is not a constraint — it is the aesthetic.

## **THE EMOTIONAL PROMISE**

You will laugh at a first prompt going spectacularly wrong. You will find yourself at 2am reading a message between strangers on opposite sides of the planet. You will finish this film convinced that the future belongs to whoever is brave enough to show up and try.

# **Chapter 02 — THE STORY ARC**

Narrative Structure  |  Three-Act Story Arc  |  1 × 60 Minutes

## **ACT ONE  |  ‘WHO ARE WE?’  —  Minutes 0–18**

**THEME: The audacity of showing up**

We open with five confessionals. ‘I’ve never written code.’ ‘My kids think I’ve lost the plot.’ Cross-cut, funny, warm, intimate. We establish the 100X Engineers program — the scale of it, the geographical spread, the improbability of this group finding each other. Then we meet our five protagonists, each in their own world.

Act 1 ends on a question: Can any of us actually do this?

## **ACT TWO  |  ‘THE MIDDLE IS WHERE YOU FIND OUT’  —  Minutes 18–46**

**THEME: The cost of transformation  |  Split: Act 2A (The Grind, min 18–33) / Midpoint Crisis (min 33\) / Act 2B (The Turn, min 33–46)**

Act 2A: The initial energy fades. Each protagonist hits their own wall. The Skeptic’s certainty cracks. The Struggler considers quitting. The Narrator tries to film and build simultaneously — the camera becomes both alibi and mirror.

Midpoint (minute 33): A collective low point. The question ‘can any of us actually do this?’ becomes urgent.

Act 2B: Something shifts. Five breakthroughs, all different, all earned. The prototypes start working. The apps take shape. Act 2 ends with everyone moving toward the showcase — but not all of them confident they’ll make it.

## **ACT THREE  |  ‘WHAT WE BECAME’  —  Minutes 46–60**

**THEME: The thing you built is not the point**

The async showcase: five apps, five self-recorded presentations, thirty people watching each other’s screens from six countries. Then the morning after — confessionals that mirror the cold open. The coda: the narrator, alone, answers the question she asked everyone else at the start.

Final image: a phone screen. A live app. A name the world doesn’t know yet.

## **TONAL REFERENCE POINTS**

* The human ensemble journey — Free Solo, The Last Dance

* The technology access story — Coded Bias, AlphaGo

* The intimate self-shot format — Tantura, Flee

# **Chapter 07A — PROTAGONIST SELECTION RUBRIC**

**HOW TO USE:**

After all 30 Stage 1 responses are in, Munish et all  read them independently first — then together. Score each respondent against the criteria below. Shortlist anyone scoring 15+ for Stage 2\. Aim for 8–10 candidates. Final five selected after Stage 2 responses and Munish selection conversations.

## **SCORING CRITERIA (25 points total)**

| CRITERION | WHAT TO LOOK FOR | SCORE (0–5) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1\. Emotional Specificity | Do they give real, specific, personal answers — or polished, generic ones? A specific fear is worth more than a vague one. A named person in the stakes question is worth more than ‘my family’. | \_\_ / 5 |
| 2\. The Gap | Is there a clear, interesting gap between who they are now and who they want to be? The bigger and more specific the gap, the stronger the arc. | \_\_ / 5 |
| 3\. Contrast Potential | Do they offer something different from the other high-scorers? We need contrast in the ensemble — age, country, archetype, starting point. Score higher if they fill a gap in the current shortlist. | \_\_ / 5 |
| 4\. Surprise Factor | Did any answer genuinely surprise you? Did they subvert an assumption? The wildcard protagonist comes from here. | \_\_ / 5 |
| 5\. Protagonist Opt-In Quality | Did they opt in to protagonist consideration? If yes — did their opt-in feel considered and genuine, or perfunctory? Reluctant opt-ins can be as interesting as enthusiastic ones. | \_\_ / 5 |

**THE NARRATOR’S ROLE IN THIS FILM:**

Shefali and Munish are not omniscient narrators standing outside the story. They are participant narrators — who are living the story and telling it at the same time. Their narration voices are intimate, wry, honest, and slightly behind the events — as if they are writing in a journal the night after something happened.

## **NARRATION PRINCIPLES**

* Narration fills what the camera cannot see — internal state, context, meaning.

* Narration never describes what we can already see on screen.

* Narration is both Munish and Shefali’s voices — not a documentary announcer. Write how she/he talks.

* Every line of narration should earn its place. If the picture tells it, cut the line.

* Narration at the start of acts sets context. Narration at the end of acts lands emotion.

## **THE SCRIPT FORMAT**

# **Chapter 21 — ASYNC SHOWCASE PROTOCOL**

**WHY ASYNC:**

A single simultaneous showcase across six countries and six time zones is logistically impossible to film well. The async format is both more honest to how this cohort actually exists — distributed, asynchronous, self-directed — and more filmable. Each person’s showcase moment is captured on their own terms, in their own space, when they’re ready.

## **THE TIMELINE**

| WHEN | WHAT HAPPENS | OWNER |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| M4 Week 6 | Shefali sends the Showcase Brief to all 30 cohort members | Shefali |
| M4 Weeks 7–8 | Submission window open. Cohort members record and upload their showcase videos. | All 30 participants |
| M4 Week 9 | Munish reviews all submissions. Selects protagonist showcases \+ up to 10 supporting moments. | Munish |
| M4 Week 10 | Munish flags selections to Eddie. Showcase footage enters the selects tracker (Chapter 17). | Munish \+ Eddie |
| M5 | Showcase footage woven into Act 3 cut. | Eddie \+ Munish |

## **THE SHOWCASE BRIEF — SENT TO ALL 30**

*Hi everyone,*

*You’re nearly there. In the next two weeks, we’d love you to record one final video — your showcase.*

*This is your moment to show what you built, why it matters, and what this journey has meant to you. There’s no live event, no Zoom call to stress about. Just you, your phone, and the app you made.*

## **WHAT TO INCLUDE IN YOUR SHOWCASE VIDEO**

| SECTION | WHAT TO COVER | SUGGESTED TIME |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1\. Your name and where you are | Say your name, your location, and one word for how you feel right now. | 30 seconds |
| 2\. Your app | Show us your app working. Screen record it or film your screen. Show us the thing it does. | 60–90 seconds |
| 3\. Who it’s for | Tell us about one real person this app is for. Not a demographic — a specific person. | 30–60 seconds |
| 4\. What this journey meant | Not the polished version. What actually changed for you? | 60–120 seconds |
| 5\. One thing you want to say | Anything. To the cohort, to someone watching, to yourself twelve weeks ago. | 30–60 seconds |

Total target: 4–5 minutes. Upload to your Google Drive folder by \[DATE\]. File name: FIRSTNAME-LASTNAME\_W10\_SHOWCASE\_YYYY-MM-DD.mp4

| Email | Formal documents, consent forms, legal matters, external communications | 48 hours |
| Zoom (ad hoc) | Any decision that’s taking more than 5 WhatsApp messages to resolve — call instead | Scheduled same day if urgent |

## **DECISION-MAKING PROTOCOL**

* Creative decisions (story, edit, tone): Munish decides. Shefali consults.

* Production decisions (scheduling, participant comms, prompts): Shefali decides. Munish informed.

* Financial decisions: Hansh flags to Shefali. Shefali approves under agreed threshold. Over threshold: Munish approves.

* Technical decisions (file system, delivery): Hansh decides.

* Marketing and content decisions: Yash proposes. Munish approves before any publishing.

* Any decision involving a participant’s consent or footage: Shefali and Munish jointly.

## **GANTT UPDATE PROTOCOL**

* Hansh updates the Gantt every Friday before end of day.

* Any task moving from On Track to At Risk must be flagged to Shefali by WhatsApp the same day.

* Any task moving to Overdue must be escalated to Munish immediately.

* The Gantt is the team’s single source of truth on project status. If it’s not in the Gantt, it doesn’t exist.

## **CONFLICT RESOLUTION**

Creative disagreements between Munish and Shefali: discuss once, then Munish decides. The Director has final say on all editorial matters.

Production disagreements: Shefali decides. Munish can override on story grounds only.

If a team member has a concern about workload, scope, or team dynamics: raise it directly with Munish in a bilateral call, not in the team WhatsApp.
